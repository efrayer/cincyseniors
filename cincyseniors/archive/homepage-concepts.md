# CincySeniors.org — Three Homepage Mockup Directions

A warm, trustworthy homepage for Greater Cincinnati older adults learning to use AI responsibly. Each direction below is designed around clarity, calm, and community — not startup sheen.

---

## Concept 1 — "The Front Porch"

A welcoming, neighborly homepage that feels like pulling up a rocking chair next to someone who can explain this new technology without rushing you. This is the warmest, most human-feeling of the three directions.

### Visual Style
Soft, hand-drawn, domestic warmth. Think the feeling of a well-loved community newsletter, a friendly church bulletin, or a public library reading program flyer. Generous white space, rounded corners everywhere, nothing sharp or clinical. Subtle paper-texture backgrounds and soft shadows that feel like a printed page rather than a glowing screen.

### Wireframe Structure (top to bottom)

1. Simple top bar: logo left, four large text links (Home, Learn, Use Cases, Ask a Question), phone number on the right
2. Hero: big friendly headline, one sentence of support copy, one primary button, a soft illustration of two people (one older, one younger) at a kitchen table with a laptop
3. "Start Here" three-card row: What is AI? / How to Stay Safe / Try It With Me
4. Health, Wealth, and Self section — three large panels, each with a friendly icon, plain-language summary, and "See Examples" link
5. "Watch Out For" scam-awareness strip — three real-world examples with big warning flags
6. Community credibility: logos or names of partner senior centers, libraries, churches; a quote from a local resident
7. Chat helper invitation: "Have a question? Ask our helper anytime." Large button, with a note that no signup is required
8. Footer: repeat of the main links, a phone number, and a plain-language privacy summary

### Suggested Colors

| Role | Color | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| Primary background | Warm cream (#FAF6EE) | Easy on aging eyes, reduces glare |
| Primary text | Deep charcoal (#1F1B16) | 15:1+ contrast on cream |
| Accent / CTA | Warm terracotta (#B45A3C) | Friendly, earthy, not corporate |
| Secondary accent | Soft sage (#6E8B6A) | For "safe," "verified," calm cues |
| Warning accent | Muted amber (#C98A2B) | For scam-awareness callouts |

### Typography
- Headings: a warm, rounded serif like Cooper BT, Recoleta, or Source Serif 4 at 40–56px
- Body: a highly legible humanist sans like Atkinson Hyperlegible or Source Sans 3 at 20–22px base
- Line height: 1.6 minimum
- No weights below 400; emphasis uses 600, never italics alone

### Image / Art Direction
Soft, hand-illustrated vignettes — watercolor-inspired, not flat vector. Real scenes: a grandmother on a porch with a phone, two neighbors looking at a tablet over coffee, a library desk with a printed guide. Human, diverse, unmistakably Cincinnati (a hint of a skyline in the distance, a Reds cap on a chair). Avoid glowing brains, circuits, robots, or blue gradients.

### Homepage Copy Ideas
- Hero headline: "AI, explained by neighbors — at your own pace."
- Subhead: "A friendly Cincinnati guide to using new technology safely, kindly, and on your own terms."
- Primary CTA: "Start with the basics"
- Secondary CTA: "Ask a question"
- Section lead-ins: "Here for you in Health," "Here for you in Wealth," "Here for you in Self"
- Scam-awareness heading: "Three things we want you to watch for this month"
- Footer note: "We're a local, community-run site. We don't sell your information. Ever."

### Section Order
Hero → Start Here cards → Health / Wealth / Self → Scam Awareness → Community Credibility → Chat Helper → Footer

### CTA Strategy
One primary action per section, and never more than two CTAs visible at once. Button labels describe the destination in plain words ("Read the guide," "See examples," "Ask our helper") rather than marketing phrases ("Get started," "Learn more"). Every CTA is a 60px tall button with a visible border and text at 20px minimum.

### Trust and Accessibility Features
Real names and photos of local advisors in the credibility strip. A visible phone number in the header and footer. A plain-language privacy note — not a link to a legal page, an actual sentence. Skip-to-content link. Focus states that are highly visible (a 3px terracotta outline). Text resize buttons in the header (A / A+ / A++). No animation except a gentle fade on page load. No auto-playing audio or video.

## Concept 2 — "The Reference Librarian"

A calm, authoritative homepage inspired by the feeling of walking up to the reference desk at the Cincinnati & Hamilton County Public Library and being treated like your question matters. This direction leans on the quiet credibility of civic institutions.

### Visual Style
Editorial, restrained, and bookish. Clean horizontal rules between sections, typographic hierarchy doing most of the work, minimal decoration. Feels closer to a well-designed public-service website (think a modernized AARP or a library system's adult-learning portal) than a tech product. Everything is framed, labeled, and easy to scan.

### Wireframe Structure (top to bottom)

1. Header with wordmark, a utility row (phone, text size controls, contrast toggle), and a simple horizontal menu of five items
2. Hero: a short, confident headline over a single horizontal photograph of a Cincinnati library or community room; primary and secondary buttons side by side
3. "This Week's Guide" featured card — one timely topic (e.g. "How to spot a phone-call AI scam")
4. Three-column Health / Wealth / Self directory with short descriptions and numbered article counts ("12 guides")
5. "How We Keep You Safe" strip — three icons with one sentence each: Privacy, Accuracy, No Sales
6. Ask-a-Question panel: a real search box plus a "Talk to our helper" button
7. Community partners row: organization names in a clean text-based list, not logos
8. Newsletter opt-in (monthly, print-mail option available)
9. Footer with a site map, accessibility statement link, and a "Contact a real person" phone number

### Suggested Colors

| Role | Color | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| Primary background | Warm ivory (#F7F3EC) | Paper-like, not stark white |
| Primary text | Near-black (#141414) | High contrast, serious |
| Accent / CTA | Deep teal-blue (#0E4C5A) | Civic, steady, trustworthy |
| Secondary accent | Warm brick (#8C3B2A) | Cincinnati-adjacent heritage red |
| Support / neutral | Stone gray (#6B6660) | For meta text, not body |

### Typography
- Headings: a confident transitional serif like Source Serif 4, Charter, or Lyon at 42–64px
- Body: a classic humanist sans like Source Sans 3, Inter, or IBM Plex Sans at 20px base
- Small caps used sparingly for section labels ("HEALTH · WEALTH · SELF")
- Clear type scale: 20 / 24 / 32 / 44 / 60

### Image / Art Direction
Black-and-white or softly toned photography of real Cincinnati places and people — a library reading room, a senior center computer lab, a kitchen, a porch in Hyde Park. One strong image per section, never a wallpaper of stock photos. Icons are simple, single-weight line drawings in the deep teal. No illustrations of AI itself.

### Homepage Copy Ideas
- Hero headline: "A plain-language guide to AI, made for Greater Cincinnati."
- Subhead: "Trusted information, clear examples, and a helper you can ask anything."
- Primary CTA: "Browse the guides"
- Secondary CTA: "Talk to our helper"
- Featured card label: "This Week's Guide"
- Safety strip headings: "Your Privacy," "Honest Answers," "Never for Sale"
- Newsletter pitch: "One short letter a month. Delivered by email — or by mail if you prefer."

### Section Order
Hero → This Week's Guide → Health / Wealth / Self Directory → How We Keep You Safe → Ask / Chat → Community Partners → Newsletter → Footer

### CTA Strategy
Two CTAs in the hero, one per section afterward. CTAs read like library signage ("Browse the guides," "Read this week's guide," "Find a class near you") rather than marketing prompts. Every CTA is paired with a short sentence of what will happen when you click it — no surprises.

### Trust and Accessibility Features
Visible text-size and contrast controls in the top-right, always available. An accessibility statement linked in the footer with a real commitment, not boilerplate. All images have descriptive captions, not just alt text. Every article indicates reading time and whether it's also available as audio or print. A "Request a printed copy" option on every guide. Clear, persistent breadcrumbs on inner pages.

## Concept 3 — "The Neighborhood Center"

A homepage that feels like walking into a Cincinnati Recreation Commission building or a senior center — civic, local, specific to *this* place. The most overtly Cincinnati of the three directions, with local landmarks, neighborhoods, and names woven in.

### Visual Style
Photographic, grounded, and civic. The design pulls from the warmth of community-center branding — rounded sans serif headings, photographic heroes, clear "program-style" cards. Not slick; closer to a well-made community program catalog than a tech company. Visible seams of local identity (Cincinnati skyline silhouette, neighborhood names, partner organization shoutouts).

### Wireframe Structure (top to bottom)

1. Header: wordmark with a small "Serving Greater Cincinnati since 2026" tag, a phone number, and a menu
2. Welcome hero: a photograph of real local residents at a community event, a headline, and a "What is this site?" one-paragraph intro
3. "Choose your starting point" — three big photo cards for Health, Wealth, Self, each with a neighborhood-flavored example ("Ask AI to read a Medicare letter," "Spot a Social Security scam call," "Write a birthday note to your grandkids")
4. "Classes and events in your neighborhood" — a light list of upcoming in-person sessions at libraries and senior centers across Hamilton, Clermont, and Warren counties
5. Safety spotlight: a rotating card highlighting one scam, one privacy tip, one AI-use idea, with a "Why we picked this" note
6. Stories from neighbors: two or three short quotes with first names, neighborhoods, and real photos
7. "Meet our helper" — an introduction to the chat tool with a screenshot and an explanation of what it can and can't do
8. Get-involved strip: volunteer, donate, suggest a topic
9. Footer with partner organizations, contact, and a mailing address

### Suggested Colors

| Role | Color | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| Primary background | Warm off-white (#FBF7F1) | |
| Primary text | Dark espresso (#231914) | Softer than pure black, still high contrast |
| Accent / CTA | Cincinnati red (#A8322A) | Local, civic, but dusty — not fire-engine |
| Secondary accent | River blue (#2E5C7E) | Evokes the Ohio River, steady |
| Support | Ivory card (#F1EADC) | For card backgrounds |

### Typography
- Headings: a friendly geometric sans with humanist warmth, such as Recoleta, Fraunces, or Publico at 44–60px
- Body: Source Sans 3 or Inter at 20–22px base
- Pull quotes set in italic serif at 28px with generous leading
- Neighborhood names and labels set in small caps

### Image / Art Direction
Real photography, commissioned locally where possible. Real Cincinnati residents, not stock models. Recognizable settings: the Main Library downtown, the Pleasant Ridge Rec Center, a Findlay Market bench, a kitchen in Norwood. When illustration is used, it's simple flat shapes, never stylized "AI" imagery. A hand-drawn skyline silhouette can appear as a thin divider between sections.

### Homepage Copy Ideas
- Hero headline: "Your Cincinnati guide to using AI — safely, wisely, and at your own pace."
- Subhead: "Free. Local. Made by and for older adults across Greater Cincinnati."
- Primary CTA: "See what AI can help with"
- Secondary CTA: "Find a class near me"
- Card example copy: "Health — Ask a question about a prescription before your next visit."
- Stories label: "From your neighbors"
- Helper intro: "Meet Cindy, our helper. She's an AI — and we'll show you exactly what she can and can't do."

### Section Order
Hero → Choose Your Starting Point (Health / Wealth / Self) → Classes and Events → Safety Spotlight → Stories From Neighbors → Meet Our Helper → Get Involved → Footer

### CTA Strategy
Local, concrete, and action-oriented. CTAs reference place and person ("Find a class in Clifton," "Read Mary's story," "Ask Cindy a question"). A persistent "Talk to someone — (513) xxx-xxxx" in the header anchors the civic feel. Every CTA has a clear outcome previewed beneath it in 18px text.

### Trust and Accessibility Features
Real local names, neighborhoods, and partner organizations on the homepage — no anonymous testimonials. A visible commitment: "We are not a tech company. We don't sell your data. We don't run ads." A printed-guide request option. Text-size controls, keyboard navigation, screen-reader-tested components, and a "Read this page aloud" button. The AI helper is introduced with clear limits — it tells you what it is, what it isn't, and when to call a real person instead.

## Recommendation

For a community-focused older adult audience in Greater Cincinnati, **Concept 3 — "The Neighborhood Center"** is the strongest direction, with selective elements borrowed from Concept 1.

The reason is that trust, for this audience, is built through *specificity and locality* more than through polish. Older adults deciding whether to engage with a new technology site are asking three unspoken questions: Who is behind this? Is it for people like me? Will they try to sell me something or trick me? Concept 3 answers all three on the homepage itself — with real neighborhood names, real faces, a visible phone number, in-person classes, and an explicit "we are not a tech company" commitment. That civic, locally grounded tone is harder to fake and much more credible than the best-designed generic site.

Concept 2 is a close second and would be the right pick if the site's primary role were to be a reference library rather than a community hub. Its restraint and editorial clarity are excellent, but it risks feeling impersonal for a first-time visitor who's uncertain about AI.

Concept 1 is the warmest, but its hand-drawn aesthetic can inadvertently signal "cute" or "for beginners" in a way some older adults find patronizing. Its strongest elements — the porch-and-neighbor metaphor, the kitchen-table illustrations, the plain-language privacy sentence — should be pulled into Concept 3 as accents.

A practical blend: build on Concept 3's structure and local voice, adopt Concept 1's plain-language privacy statement and warm color temperature, and take Concept 2's accessibility controls (text size, contrast, audio version) as a permanent top-bar feature. That combination gives the site the civic credibility of a community center, the neighborly warmth of a front porch, and the accessibility discipline of a public library — which is exactly the tone an older-adult AI literacy site should strike.
